Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The data is showing that in early 2020, there were very few reported cases of COVID-19, with January-March 2020 having 0 cases. But as the year progressed, the number of cases increased significantly, depicting the initial rapid spread of the virus.
  • From this visualisation, we see that cases tend to peak around November to January, which might be due to seasonal factors like colder weather, increased travel, and indoor gatherings. The accompanying article mentions that reporting of cases is often delayed during mid-December till early January and the Omicron variant wave was expected to differ from other variants, so the projections for January 2022 have a significant amount of uncertainty to them.
  • Cases seem to rapidly decline during the warmer months of May-July, and rapidly increase again in the fall. As a college student, I wonder how much of this pattern is due to students being out of school during the summer and returning - with whatever illnesses they might have picked up - in the fall.

Design Critique:

I think this visualisation is effective at showing the overall trend of COVID-19 cases over the given time frame, but it could be improved in a few ways. The spiral format was a strong visual choice, leveraging the cyclical nature of time to show the seasonal patterns in the data. Furthermore, the choice to label just the months at the cardinal locations (i.e. January, April, July, October) was a good one to avoid cluttering the visualisation and utilizing clock imagery to further reinforce the sensation of time passing. Also, after being introduced to "cones of uncertainty" from the accompanying article, I appreciate the visualisation's use of a shaded area that is often used in plots representing uncertainty, as it could remind viewers that the projections for January 2022 are not set in stone and should not be taken as a precise prediction. Although it is difficult to get exact case numbers from the visualisation, viewers are able to easily interpret the general trends and patterns in the data, which is probably more important for this type of data when presenting to a general audience. Additionally, there is no explicit label for the date but I think the use of the spiral format and the placement of the months at the cardinal locations saves this from being an issue, as it makes it clear that the data is being plotted over time.
All this being said, there are some areas that could be greatly improved. I think the visualisation could benefit from a more centralised, clearer title that explicitly states what the data is showing. The current title is off to one side which makes it seem like it is not of great importance. Also, there is no mention of the data for January 2022 and possibly late December 2021 being projections, which could lead to viewers assuming the data is more certain than it actually is. I think the visualisation could be improved by adding a clear label or annotation to indicate that the data for January 2022 is a projection and to explain the uncertainty associated with it. Additionally, choosing a different colour to represent each year in the spiral could have helped to make the trends for each year easier to distinguish. Finally, I think the visualisation could greatly benefit from annotations detailing significant events that may have influenced the trends being displayed; since the exact numbers are hard to read from the visual, ease of interpretation of general trends is key, and providing context for the trends would help viewers understand the data more deeply. Furthemore, adding annotations could create a sense of a timeline which works well with the current design choices. Cluttering is a concern to watch for, but I think a few annotations would not increase the visualisation's clutter since the visualisation is already quite sparse.

Final Visualization Design

Final Writeup

With my final design, I aimed to create a visualisation that clearly conveys the exact numbers and trends of cases of COVID-19 in the United States over time, while evoking a strong sense of the passage of time. I chose a line graph as my final visual format because I think it evokes that strong sense of time flowing without jeopardizing the clarity in which the data is presented. I chose the separate the different years by colour to make it easier to distinguish between the years without having to continually refer back to the x-axis. As in my sketches, I used the Pantone colours of the year to represent the different years to avoid any unnecessary bias in the interpretation of the data because of the colours (e.g. using red, yellow, and green assigns a sense of "better" and "worse" to the data). Additionally, I made the section of the line representing the projected number of cases for 2022 a dashed line to clearly indicate that the data is not as certain as the data for the previous years. I added an annotation to highlight when the Omicron variant was declare one for concern, as this was a significant event that likely infleunced the tends in case numbers following that point. I also added arrows to highlight the month in each year with the highest number of cases and the lowest number of cases: an upward, red arrow for the for the month with the highest number of cases and a downward, green arrow for the month with the lowest number of cases. With these arrows, I opted to lean into the preconceived notions associated with the colours red and green because I think their intuitive nature allowed me to convey the message I wanted to without needing to add extra explanatory text that would have cluttered the visualisation. I kept my chart title and axis labels simple and straightforward to maintain the clarity of the visualisation, but I made sure to center the title and make it larger than the axis labels to give it more prominence and make it clear to viewers what the data is showing from the moment they start interacting with the visualisation.

I think my final design does well in building on what worked in the original visualisation, and improving what didn't. In terms of what worked, I kept the the visual theme of time passing that was so effective in the original visualisation, but I used a different visual format that I think is even more effective at conveying that theme while also being more clear and informative. I also prioritised illustrating the trends in the cases, and added an element of uncertainty with the dotted line for the projected data, which were both things I appreciated about the original visualisation. In my final design, I think I was able to successfully address the issues I had with the original visualisation by centering the title, adding annotations to aid viewers in interpreting the data, explicitly stating that the data for 2022 was based on projections, and using distinct colours to represent the different years. One tradeoff I encountered was between visual appeal and clarity. I tried to explore different visuals that were as visually interesting and eye-catching as the original visulisation with my sketches, but I found that many of the more visually interesting formats I came up with were not as effective at clearly conveying the data and trends so I ultimately decided to go with a more conventional format for my final design. Coming up with annotations was also more difficult than I anticipated, as I had to be careful to only include annotations that were indisputably linked to the trends in the data and did not aim to push viewers towards any particular interpretation of the data (I had wanted to include an annotation in 2020 for when the first vaccine was released but I ultimately decided against it because vaccines and their effectiveness can be a controversial topic and I didn't want my annotation to indicate a stance on the topic). Overall, I am satisfied with my final design and I greatly appreciate how the process of critiquing and redesigning the original visualisation allowed me to iterate on my notion of what was most important to convey with the data and how to best do that.
